Children ask more questions when they think their teacher is AI

Blerim Jashari

Ilona Bass, Tomer Ullman, Elizabeth Bonawitz, Candice M. Mills

EDU S802 · Spring 2026

Questions are important

Questions are important

  • For scientific inquiry: which can be understood as a systematic process of asking questions and seeking answers.
  • For everyday life: we ask questions to navigate decisions, understand others, and make sense of new situations.
  • For learning: by asking questions, children can resolve misunderstandings, reduce uncertainty, and build causal knowledge that helps them make sense of the world.

(Jirout & Klahr, 2011; Park et al., 2025)

But questions have a social cost

  • Posing a question can signal to others that you do not know something.
  • When children enter school, they begin to ask fewer questions.
  • By around age 7, children begin to associate asking for help with appearing less competent.
  • Children may therefore avoid asking questions if they feel they are being judged, even when asking would help them learn.

(Tizard & Hughes, 1984; Engel, 2011; Harter, 2012; Elkind & Bowen, 1979; Somerville et al., 2013; Leary & Kowalski, 1990; Good & Shaw, 2022)

Judgment requires a judge

But what if the listener
can't judge you or you don't care if it does?

Research Question

Do children (ages 7–12) ask more questions and learn more when they believe they are interacting with an AI chatbot vs. a human teacher?

Method

  • Science Lessons: children listen to challenging lessons about four body organs.
  • Question-Asking: children ask questions to Vera (AI Chatbot or Human Teacher).
  • Assessment: learning test and appraisal questions.

Science Lessons

Children listen to intentionally challenging lessons about four body organs:

Brain

Heart

Lungs

Kidneys

Example: The Kidneys

Question-Asking Phase

AI Chatbot

Now you ask questions about the lesson to: Vera who is an AI Computer Program developed from BrightRiver Lab in Massachusetts. It was developed for kids around your age and help them with their science questions.

Human Teacher

Now you can ask questions about the lesson to: Vera who is a Teacher from BrightRiver Elementary School in Massachusetts. She works with kids around your age and helps them with their science questions.

How Vera Answers

Children believe Vera is responding to them in real time. Behind the scenes, we play pre-recorded answers identical across all participants.

"What does murky mean?"

If a child asks a question we have not anticipated, we generate it live using a custom GPT, then save it for future participants.

"Is one lung smaller than the other?"

Assessment: Learning Questions

20-item learning test (scored 0–2 each) assessing three types of understanding. For example, for kidneys:

Main function

Scientific terms & analogies

Explain in own words

Transfer to new situations

Assessment: Appraisals

We ask children about their experience with Vera across five dimensions: comfort, ease, understanding, belief/trust, and liking.

Binary response

3-point scale

Measures

  • Number of Questions
  • Learning Score
  • Appraisals

While not analyzed for this preliminary analysis, we also measure the type of questions children ask, such as fact-based vs. explanation-seeking questions, and whether questions are related to the lesson or not.

Other Results

  • No other appraisal measures reached statistical significance between conditions.
  • Mediation analysis showed no significant indirect effect of questions on learning (ACME = −0.46, 95% CI [−1.64, 1.18], p = .51). The direct effect of condition on learning remained significant (p = .027).
  • Age was positively correlated with learning (r = .56, p < .001) but not with questions asked (r = .04, p = .83).

Discussion

  • These results do not necessarily mean that AI is a better teacher.
  • Rather, AI may support learning by reducing the social costs of revealing what one doesn't know.
  • While this study focuses on children and question-asking, these findings may extend to other contexts governed by the same social forces, such as shame and fear of judgment.

Future Directions

  • Reach target N = 100.
  • Expand to teenagers and adults.
  • Does it matter if the AI has human-like attributes versus not?
  • How is feedback received differently when it comes from an AI, a teacher, or a peer?
